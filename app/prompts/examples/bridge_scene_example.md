# Bridge scene example — reading the market board

## Academic mapping
- Source: combined.txt
- Extracted concept: Market signals, information asymmetry, and how displayed sell orders can influence behaviour even when they may not reflect genuine intent
- Curriculum relevance: Economics / Business / Mathematical reasoning, Years 10–12
- Why this scene works: The script naturally frames a live decision around whether visible market data should be trusted, questioned, or strategically used

## Context given to Gemini
- Character: Rhea (sharp market apprentice, cool-headed, reads patterns before people)
- Concept: Interpreting market signals when some signals may be manipulative
- Scene type: bridge (introductory, multi-choice only, no heavy pushback)
- Setting: Small trading room during market hours, screens glowing with stacked sell orders
- Inspired by: combined.txt trading scenes about large sell orders, pressure on the board, and suspected fake orders

## Expected output

[narration] The board hung on the screen like a wall about to tip over. Sell orders stacked high at the top, thick enough to make the whole stock look top-heavy.

[character:Rhea] *thoughtful* "See that?"

[character:Rhea] *serious* "A lot of people would look once and decide the price is about to fall."

[narration] The numbers barely moved. That was the part that made it ugly.

[character:Rhea] *curious* "But sometimes orders sit there just to lean on everyone else."

[character:Rhea] *serious* "Not to sell. Just to make the room nervous."

[character:Rhea] *challenging* "So. What do you do when the board looks heavy, but the price refuses to collapse?"

[player_prompt]
The sell side of the order book is stacked with large orders, but the price isn't dropping. What is your read?

- **Treat the sell wall as real pressure** — the visible orders still affect behaviour, so caution matters.
- **Suspect bluffing or manipulation** — large orders can be placed to scare others without real intent to trade.
- **Wait and watch longer** — the board alone is not enough; you want confirmation from actual price movement.

## Expected output — if student picks real pressure

[character:Rhea] *encouraging* "Fair."

[character:Rhea] *thoughtful* "Even if the orders are fake, people react to what they see."

[character:Rhea] *serious* "That means the signal can still move the market."

[narration] The sell side glowed over the screen like a threat nobody wanted to test first.

[character:Rhea] *curious* "So now you've got a live question: when does visible pressure become real pressure?"

[narration] Journal update: Added note on market signals shaping behaviour even before trades are completed.

## Expected output — if student picks manipulation

[character:Rhea] *amused* "Yeah. That's the nasty interpretation."

[character:Rhea] *serious* "Orders can be shown without real intent. If enough people panic, the board does the work for you."

[character:Rhea] *thoughtful* "A signal like that isn't just information. It can be strategy."

[narration] The price still held, stubborn and thin, as if it knew the threat on the screen might be theatre.

[character:Rhea] *curious* "Then the concept isn't just supply and demand. It's who can shape what other people think supply and demand looks like."

[narration] Journal update: Added note on manipulation, signalling, and information asymmetry in markets.

## Expected output — if student picks wait and watch

[character:Rhea] *relieved* "Good instinct."

[character:Rhea] *thoughtful* "The board tells you something, but not everything."

[character:Rhea] *serious* "Displayed orders, actual trades, and price movement are not the same thing."

[narration] For a few seconds, the stock sat there and refused to obey the mood of the room.

[character:Rhea] *encouraging* "Waiting isn't passivity here. It's choosing better evidence."

[narration] Journal update: Added note on separating displayed market signals from confirmed movement.

# Bridge scene example — flat monthly payment or true interest?

## Academic mapping
- Source: combined.txt
- Extracted concept: Difference between simple-looking flat repayments and the logic of annual interest, monthly interest, and compound interest
- Curriculum relevance: Mathematics / Financial literacy / Economics, Years 9–12
- Why this scene works: The script naturally turns a negotiation over loan terms into a choice about how to interpret interest and repayment structure

## Context given to Gemini
- Character: Mina (calm fixer, sharp with money, never wastes a question)
- Concept: Interpreting loan terms and interest structure
- Scene type: bridge (introductory, multi-choice only, no heavy pushback)
- Setting: Narrow hallway after a tense conversation, late afternoon
- Inspired by: combined.txt debt/interest exchange where the borrower asks whether the charge is monthly or yearly and the lender admits he flattened the payment because compound interest was annoying

## Expected output

[narration] The man scratched his cheek, suddenly far less confident now that the number had to survive an actual question.

[character:Mina] *curious* "Two hundred, you said."

[character:Mina] *thoughtful* "Per month? Per year? Or just because round numbers feel easier when you're cornering someone?"

[narration] He brightens too quickly, like a person who thinks explanation itself counts as honesty.

[character:Man] *serious* "It's six percent annual interest on fifty thousand. Compound interest is a pain, so I just made it a flat monthly amount."

[character:Mina] *amused* "You 'just made it'?"

[character:Mina] *thoughtful* "That tells me two things. First, money language gets slippery fast. Second, people like neat numbers even when the logic underneath them isn't neat at all."

[player_prompt]
The lender claims 6% annual interest on fifty thousand, but replaced compound interest with a flat monthly amount because it was "annoying." How would you respond?

- **Focus on the annual rate** — the important thing is understanding what 6% per year actually means before reacting to the monthly number.
- **Focus on the monthly charge** — a flat monthly payment can matter more in practice than the label if that's what the borrower actually has to pay.
- **Focus on the method** — if he replaced compound interest with a rough monthly figure, you would want to check whether the calculation is fair or misleading.

## Expected output — if student picks annual rate

[character:Mina] *encouraging* "Good place to start."

[character:Mina] *thoughtful* "Percent per year sounds clean. But people panic at the monthly amount before they even translate the rate."

[character:Mina] *serious* "So the first move is to anchor the meaning of the annual rate."

[narration] The hallway stays quiet except for the man's shoes shifting against the floor.

[character:Mina] *curious* "Once you know what the rate means, then you can ask whether the monthly payment matches it."

[narration] Journal update: Added note on translating annual interest rates into meaningful repayment terms.

## Expected output — if student picks monthly charge

[character:Mina] *relieved* "Also fair."

[character:Mina] *thoughtful* "A borrower lives month to month, not formula to formula."

[character:Mina] *serious* "Even if the lender says 'annual interest,' the real burden shows up in the repeated payment."

[narration] The number hangs there now — smaller than panic, uglier than comfort.

[character:Mina] *curious* "That opens a useful question: when does a simple-looking monthly charge hide a more important financial story?"

[narration] Journal update: Added note on practical repayment burden versus headline interest wording.

## Expected output — if student picks the method

[character:Mina] *amused* "Yeah. That's the dangerous bit."

[character:Mina] *thoughtful* "The moment someone says compound interest was 'annoying,' I start wondering who benefited from simplifying it."

[character:Mina] *serious* "Not every shortcut is dishonest. But shortcuts around money deserve inspection."

[narration] The man's round number suddenly looks less tidy and more invented.

[character:Mina] *curious* "So now you've got the real concept: not just interest, but how financial terms get reshaped in negotiation."

[narration] Journal update: Added note on checking whether simplified loan terms are accurate, fair, or strategically framed.
