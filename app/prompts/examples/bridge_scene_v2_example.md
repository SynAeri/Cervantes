# Bridge Scene V2 Example — Economic Indicators

## Context
- Character: Anya (economics student, overwhelmed by conflicting data)
- Concept: GDP, inflation, and unemployment as interconnected indicators
- Scene type: bridge (introductory with conditional pushback)
- Setting: Campus library, late afternoon

---

## Scene Content

[narration] The library table is covered in printouts — graphs trending up, down, sideways. Anya stares at three different charts like they're arguing with each other.

[character:Anya] [concerned] GDP is up, but unemployment isn't dropping.

[character:Anya] [neutral] And inflation keeps climbing even though people say the economy is growing.

[narration] She taps one of the charts, then another, as if physical contact might force them to agree.

[character:Anya] [challenging] These indicators tell a story, but this one's a mess.

[player_prompt] Anya's overwhelmed by the conflicting signals. What's the best approach to make sense of these economic indicators for her startup's market analysis?
**A. Prioritize GDP growth** – It's the most comprehensive measure of overall economic activity and potential market size.
**B. Focus on inflation and unemployment** – These directly impact consumer purchasing power and operational costs for a new business.
**C. Look for the interplay between all three** – No single indicator tells the full story; they often influence each other.

## Expected output — if student picks A
[character:Anya] [neutral] I suppose focusing on GDP makes sense for the big picture.

[character:Anya] [concerned] But wait... if GDP is growing but unemployment stays high, doesn't that mean the growth isn't reaching everyone?

[character:Anya] [challenging] And if inflation is rising faster than GDP, isn't that eating away at the "growth"?

[player_prompt] Anya's noticing something GDP alone doesn't show. What's missing from just looking at GDP growth?
**A. How the growth is distributed across the population** – High unemployment means growth isn't creating enough jobs.
**B. The real purchasing power after inflation** – Nominal growth doesn't mean real improvement if prices rise faster.
**C. Both distribution and purchasing power** – GDP is a useful starting point, but you need the other indicators to complete the picture.

## Expected output — if student picks A then C
[character:Anya] [encouraging] Okay, so GDP tells me the size of the pie, but inflation and unemployment tell me who's actually getting fed and how much that food costs them.

[character:Anya] [surprised] It's not that GDP is wrong — it's that it's incomplete without the other pieces!

[character:Anya] [neutral] For my startup analysis, I need to look at all three together to understand the real market environment.

[narration] Journal update: Understood that GDP, inflation, and unemployment are interconnected indicators providing a complete economic picture when analyzed together.

## Expected output — if student picks B
[character:Anya] [encouraging] You're right, I've been so focused on the 'big picture' I forgot about the immediate impact on people!

[character:Anya] [neutral] If inflation is high, our customers have less discretionary income, and our material costs could skyrocket.

[character:Anya] [thoughtful] And if unemployment isn't improving, who's going to buy our product? These figures directly affect our target market and our potential employee pool.

[character:Anya] [challenging] But here's what I'm still confused about — why would GDP be growing if these other indicators are struggling?

[character:Anya] [concerned] Is GDP growth without employment growth even... real growth?

[narration] Journal update: Recognized inflation and unemployment as crucial indicators impacting consumer spending and business costs, with growing awareness of how they relate to GDP.

## Expected output — if student picks C
[character:Anya] [surprised] Oh, thank goodness, it's not just me who thinks they're connected!

[character:Anya] [neutral] My brain keeps trying to untangle them, but they just get more tangled.

[character:Anya] [encouraging] So, instead of seeing them as contradictory, I need to see how they influence each other?

[character:Anya] [thoughtful] Like, strong GDP growth **should** eventually bring down unemployment, but if inflation is high, that growth might feel less impactful for consumers.

[character:Anya] [neutral] It's a balance. No single number tells the full story.

[narration] Journal update: Understood that economic indicators like GDP, inflation, and unemployment are interconnected and provide a more complete economic picture when analyzed together.

# Bridge Scene V2 Example — Opportunity Cost

## Context
- Character: Marcus (food truck owner, making expansion decisions)
- Concept: Opportunity cost in resource allocation
- Scene type: bridge (introductory with conditional pushback)
- Setting: Food truck parking lot, evening

---

## Scene Content

[narration] The parking lot is quiet now. Marcus leans against his food truck, counting the day's cash with one hand and scrolling through a bank loan offer with the other.

[character:Marcus] [concerned] I've got ten thousand saved. Bank's offering me a loan for another fifteen at decent rates.

[character:Marcus] [neutral] I could upgrade the kitchen equipment — better grill, faster fryer. Or I could use it as a down payment on a second truck.

[narration] He looks at the truck like it's both his pride and his cage.

[character:Marcus] [challenging] Different moves, different futures. And once I pick one, the other one's gone.

[player_prompt] Marcus has to choose between upgrading his current truck's equipment or using the money as a down payment on a second truck. What should he consider?
**A. Which option makes more money** – Compare potential revenue increase from each choice.
**B. Which option he prefers personally** – His comfort with managing two trucks versus improving one.
**C. What he's giving up by not choosing the other** – The opportunity cost of each decision.

## Expected output — if student picks A
[character:Marcus] [encouraging] Yeah, money talks. Obviously.

[character:Marcus] [thoughtful] Better equipment means I can serve more customers faster. Second truck means double the locations, double the revenue.

[character:Marcus] [challenging] But here's the thing — if I buy the second truck, I'm not just spending fifteen grand. I'm also **not** spending it on equipment.

[character:Marcus] [concerned] So the real cost of the second truck isn't just the cash I spend. It's also the revenue I'm giving up by not upgrading this one.

[player_prompt] Marcus is realizing the cost isn't just the money spent. What's the term for what you give up when you choose one option over another?
**A. Sunk cost** – Money already spent that can't be recovered.
**B. Opportunity cost** – The value of the best alternative you didn't choose.
**C. Marginal cost** – The cost of producing one more unit.

## Expected output — if student picks A then B
[character:Marcus] [surprised] Opportunity cost. Right.

[character:Marcus] [neutral] It's not about what I spend. It's about what I don't get because I spent it somewhere else.

[character:Marcus] [encouraging] So when I'm comparing these options, I need to think about both what I gain **and** what I'm giving up.

[narration] Journal update: Understood opportunity cost as the value of the best alternative foregone when making a choice.

## Expected output — if student picks B
[character:Marcus] [neutral] I mean, yeah, I need to be comfortable with the choice.

[character:Marcus] [concerned] But comfort doesn't pay the bills. And it doesn't tell me if I'm making a smart move.

[character:Marcus] [challenging] What if I love the idea of two trucks, but upgrading one truck would actually make me more money?

[character:Marcus] [thoughtful] Then my "preference" just cost me profit. That's not a decision — that's a daydream.

[character:Marcus] [neutral] I need to think about what I'm actually giving up with each choice, not just what I'm getting.

[narration] Journal update: Noted that personal preference alone doesn't account for opportunity cost in business decisions.

## Expected output — if student picks C
[character:Marcus] [surprised] Exactly! You get it.

[character:Marcus] [encouraging] It's not just about what I buy. It's about what I **don't** buy.

[character:Marcus] [thoughtful] If I upgrade the equipment, I'm giving up the chance to expand to two locations. If I get the second truck, I'm giving up faster service and higher capacity here.

[character:Marcus] [neutral] That's opportunity cost — the value of the best thing I'm **not** choosing.

[character:Marcus] [challenging] And the tricky part? I need to actually estimate what that "value" is. Not just gut feeling — real numbers.

[narration] Journal update: Demonstrated understanding of opportunity cost as the value of the best alternative foregone in resource allocation decisions.
